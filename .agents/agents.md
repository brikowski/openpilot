## Submodule & Branch Mechanics
- **Submodule Flow**: Vehicle platform logic is located in the [opendbc_repo](file:///Users/travisbadgley/openpilot/opendbc_repo) submodule. Edits must be committed inside `opendbc_repo/` first, and the submodule tracking pointer must then be updated and committed in the parent `openpilot` repository.
- **NEVER sync opendbc to `opendbc upstream/master` — pin it to the commit openpilot master pins**: the two upstream repos drift, and `opendbc/car/car.capnp` defines the `CarParams` schema that openpilot's Python reads via capnp. Squashing our tune onto opendbc *master HEAD* while the parent sits on openpilot master ships a mismatched pair and **crashes `controlsd` on-road**. This actually happened 2026-07-18: opendbc `2ecf53cf8` ("Hyundai: remove explicit longitudinal starting state", #3562) moved `startingState` into a nested `deprecated` group, so `longcontrol.py`'s `CP.startingState` raised `AttributeError: struct has no such member`. (Note it was #3562, not #3559 - #3559 created the deprecated-group *style* but left `startingState` top-level. comma demotes a field only in the same commit that removes its last consumer, which is exactly why the pin is always safe and opendbc master is not.) controlsd died -> `processNotRunning` -> `accFaulted` -> **`relayMalfunction`**. Note the presenting symptom was a *harness relay error*, three layers downstream of the real cause — always read the rlog traceback (`errorLogMessage` where `msg == "crash"`, field `exc_info`) before believing the alert. Correct procedure: `PINNED=$(git ls-tree upstream/master opendbc_repo | awk '{print $3}')` in the parent, then squash/rebase opendbc onto `$PINNED`. The "Sync Upstream Codebase" task now does this. Verify after any sync that `git -C opendbc_repo diff --stat $PINNED..ody-op-long` shows **only** our tuned car files.

## Custom Tuning & Development Guidelines
- **Prioritize Stock Safety**: Always prioritize what Comma does natively. Stock openpilot behavior and safety mechanisms should be trusted over custom hacks. If something is behaving poorly, look for tuning parameters (like PI gains or deadzones) rather than overriding the physics math or disabling safety nets.
- **Cross-Brand Comparison**: Before proposing custom fixes for a specific car, always cross-reference how openpilot handles similar edge cases for other vehicle brands.
- **Planner/Perception vs. Car-Port Boundary**: Late or hard braking on a lead approach, lead-tracking dropouts (`radarState/leadOne/dRel` jumping to max and back), and jumpy `aTarget` are **upstream of the car port** - they come from the model / `radard` / longitudinal planner, not `carcontroller`. Diagnose before tuning: if `aEgo` faithfully tracks `aTarget`, the control is fine and it's the planner/perception; only if the *output* diverges from the *command* is it ours. Don't chase planner behavior in the tune. The real lever is **driving personality** (Relaxed = longer follow distance, earlier and gentler braking). This has recurred many times in this project - verify the trace first.
- **Document All Custom Changes**: Every single custom edit must include an inline comment explaining exactly *why* the change was made, followed by a strict `TODO: delete excessive comments before trying to submit a PR.`. The custom tuning logic is the actual contribution and is meant to stay; it's the verbose journal-style commentary that gets trimmed down before upstream submission, so the branch stays clean without losing the tune.
- **Write Findings Into the Code, Not Just the Chat**: When a session spends real effort (WebFetch calls, DBC spelunking, log analysis) establishing *why* something is tuned a certain way, that reasoning belongs in a comment at the point of use, not just in conversation - it saves re-deriving the same investigation (and the tokens/PR fetches that cost) in a future session.
- **Comments Are a Starting Point, Not Ground Truth**: Custom-tune comments (including "CUSTOM TUNE" blocks and any journal-style writeups) reflect the reasoning *at the time they were written*. Treat them as a lead to verify, not a fact to cite - upstream PRs move, DBC signals get re-checked, and code gets reworked or reverted out from under a comment that still references it. If you find one that's stale, wrong, or points at code that no longer exists, correct or remove it as part of your change rather than leaving it to mislead the next session.
- **Jotpluggler Layout**: The `brikowski` layout (`openpilot/tools/jotpluggler/layouts/brikowski.json`, launched via the "Run Jotpluggler" VS Code task) is the standard layout for reviewing tuning drives. Current tabs: **Lateral - Core** and **Lateral - States** (curvature/lateral-accel tracking, `torqueState`, the `saturated` flag); **Long - Tracking** (`aTarget` vs `aEgo`, and `actuators/accel` vs `actuatorsOutput/accel` - if command and output diverge, suspect the `brake_pid`; if `aTarget` itself is late/jumpy, it's the planner); **Long - Learning** (the live factors - `actuatorsOutput/gas` is repurposed to the *effective* gasfactor and `/brake` to windfactor; watch these converge and stay stable across drives); **Long - CAN Ground Truth** (raw `ACC_CONTROL`/`GAS_PEDAL_2`/`ENGINE_DATA` signals for Bosch A cars like the Odyssey 5G MMR); **Live Parameters** (steerRatio, latAccelFactor, lateralDelay, roll); and **Lead Dynamics & Feedforward** (lead-relative tracking + pitch feedforward, for isolating planner/perception vs. control-loop issues).

## Known Upstream Constraints (Honda Bosch A/C - not cached locally, re-fetch if reasoning needs re-verifying)
- **opendbc PR #2165** (github.com/commaai/opendbc/pull/2165): wind drag + hill/pitch compensation for Bosch gas pedal force. Still draft upstream, parked pending a broader drivetrain-torque refactor.
- **opendbc PR #2347** (github.com/commaai/opendbc/pull/2347): documents that Honda Bosch's own ECU already runs an internal brake PID. Stock `kp=0, ki=0` (pure feedforward) in `interface.py` is deliberate - adding openpilot's own closed-loop kp/ki on top "doubles up... and causes oscillating braking/acceleration strength." Check this before adding closed-loop longitudinal gain on a Honda Bosch car.
- **opendbc PR #2767** (github.com/commaai/opendbc/pull/2767, closed): a comma engineer tried pitch-compensation on the gas pedal and hit "will need to switch the gas actuator from accel-based to torque-based first." Bosch A has no writable torque CAN signal (`ACC_CONTROL.ACCEL_COMMAND` is a real m/s2 value Honda's ECU closes its own loop on; `ACC_CONTROL.GAS_COMMAND` is opaque/unitless). A torque-based redesign would mean reverse-engineering a speed-dependent `GAS_COMMAND`-to-torque calibration using the car's own `GAS_PEDAL_2.ENGINE_TORQUE_ESTIMATE` telemetry as ground truth.
- **Current longitudinal design on this branch**: `ody-op-long` runs a live-learning gas feedforward plus a supplemental brake. It is the reworked "best version" of an earlier design that was briefly removed 2026-07-14 - do NOT delete it thinking it's the old buggy one; verify against `carcontroller.py`. Pieces: (1) a speed-scheduled `gasfactor` baseline (`GAS_FACTOR_SPEED_BP/V`, a check-mark shape - high at launch, dip at low cruise, rising with speed) with a live-learned residual `gasfactor` trim on top (`effective = base(vEgo) * trim`); (2) a `windfactor` aero-drag term; (3) grade (`hill_brake = sin(pitch)*g`) + drag feedforward that feed the **gas** side only; (4) a supplemental integral-only `brake_pid` (the one term touching `ACCEL_COMMAND`). Why it's safe where the old one wasn't: it never stacks openpilot `kp/ki` on Honda's ECU brake PID (the #2347 risk stays avoided), the `brake_pid` is gated on `gas_pedal_force` (not raw accel) so it can't wind up unused brake while in the gas domain, the low-speed gas/brake switch uses planner accel to hold the brake through a stop, and the original's uninitialized-attribute bug is fixed. Keep this architecture.
